Nature of Management — Is it Science or Art?

Management as Science
  • Systematised body of knowledge
  • Universal principles (Fayol's principles)
  • Cause-and-effect relationships
  • Tested through observation
Management as Art
  • Personalised application of knowledge
  • Requires creativity & judgment
  • Improves with practice
  • Goal-oriented
Examiner note: Management is both science AND art — mention this nuance. It is an "inexact science" — principles are guidelines, not rigid laws.

Part 2 — Fayol's 14 Principles of Management

Classical Theory · Most Frequently Tested · 15-marker 2023
🔥 PYQ ALERT — 15 Marks (2023)

"Discuss the 14 Principles of Management given by Henri Fayol. How are these principles relevant to modern management?" — This was a full 15-marker. Know ALL 14 cold with examples.

Henri Fayol — Background

  • French industrialist — worked at Comambault Mining Company (rose to Director General)
  • Published "Administration Industrielle et Générale" (1916) — English: "General and Industrial Management"
  • Father of Modern Management Theory / Classical Administrative Theory
  • Focus: Entire organisation (top-down) vs Taylor's shop floor focus
  • Identified 6 activities of industrial firms: Technical, Commercial, Financial, Security, Accounting, Managerial
Mnemonic for 14 principles: Division Authority Discipline Unity Unity Subordination Remuneration Centralisation Scalar Order Equity Stability Initiative Esprit
→ DADUUS R CSS OESIE — or group them: 1-4 (structure), 5-7 (authority/pay), 8-10 (organisation), 11-14 (people)
Principle 1
Division of Work
Specialisation increases efficiency. Each person focuses on a specific task. Applies to technical AND managerial work.
Ex: Assembly line — each worker does one task
Principle 2
Authority & Responsibility
Authority = right to give orders. Responsibility = obligation to perform. Authority and responsibility must be equal and commensurate.
Ex: A manager given power to hire must be accountable for team output
Principle 3
Discipline
Obedience, application, energy, behaviour. Requires good supervision, fair agreements, and judicious penalties.
Ex: Attendance rules, code of conduct
Principle 4
Unity of Command
An employee should receive orders from ONE superior only. Multiple bosses = confusion, conflict, undermined authority.
Ex: No employee reports to two managers simultaneously
Principle 5
Unity of Direction
One head, one plan for a group of activities with the same objective. Related to organisation, not individual employees.
Ex: All marketing activities under one Marketing Manager with one plan
Principle 6
Subordination of Individual Interest
Individual/group interests must be subordinated to organisational interest. Organisation's interest is supreme.
Ex: Employee skipping personal plans for critical project deadline
Principle 7
Remuneration of Personnel
Compensation must be fair and satisfactory to both employees and employers. Avoid over/under payment. Methods: time rate, job rate, profit sharing.
Ex: Performance-linked pay ensuring motivation
Principle 8
Centralisation
Degree to which authority is concentrated at top. Not absolute — optimal balance of centralisation vs decentralisation depends on organisation size.
Ex: Startup → high centralisation; Large MNC → decentralised
Principle 9
Scalar Chain
Chain of superiors from highest to lowest authority. Communication flows along this chain. Gang Plank: horizontal communication between same-level managers (Fayol allowed this for emergencies).
Ex: Org chart hierarchy; direct communication between peers for urgency
Principle 10
Order
Material Order: a place for everything and everything in its place. Social Order: right person in the right place.
Ex: Filing system, job descriptions matching skills
Principle 11
Equity
Combination of kindness and justice. Equal and fair treatment of all employees. Promotes loyalty and devotion.
Ex: No discrimination by gender/caste in promotions
Principle 12
Stability of Tenure
Employee turnover is harmful. Time needed to settle into job. Avoid frequent transfers/dismissals. Mediocre stable manager > brilliant unstable one.
Ex: Job security policies, low attrition culture
Principle 13
Initiative
Freedom to conceive and execute plans. Must be encouraged within limits of authority and discipline. Enhances zeal and energy.
Ex: Google's 20% time policy for personal projects
Principle 14
Esprit de Corps
Team spirit — harmony and union among personnel. "Union is strength." Avoid divide-and-rule, excessive written communication.
Ex: Team celebrations, collaborative culture
Unity of Command vs Unity of Direction — Most Confused Pair:
Unity of Command = one boss per employee (individual level)
Unity of Direction = one plan per activity group (organisational level)
Exam trap: "Marketing team under one marketing plan" = Unity of Direction (NOT Command)

Part 3 — Taylor's Scientific Management

Classical Theory · Father of Scientific Management

F.W. Taylor — Background

  • American mechanical engineer at Midvale Steel Company (1878) → Bethlehem Steel
  • Published "Principles of Scientific Management" (1911)
  • Father of Scientific Management / Shop-floor management
  • Focus: Eliminating "soldiering" (deliberate slow work by workers)
  • Developed Time and Motion Study

Taylor's 5 Principles of Scientific Management

Mnemonic: S-S-C-S-H — "Scientists Scientifically Create Scientific Humans"
#PrincipleMeaning
1Science, not Rule of ThumbReplace guesswork with scientifically determined methods. Each task analysed scientifically.
2Scientific Selection & TrainingSelect workers based on skills needed. Train them scientifically for specific tasks.
3Cooperation, not DiscordMental revolution — both management and workers must understand each other's roles. Harmony over conflict.
4Scientific Division of WorkEqual division of work between management and workers. Management plans; workers execute.
5Maximum OutputReplace restricted output with maximum output. More production = more prosperity for both.

Taylor's 5 Techniques

TechniqueDescription
Time StudyDetermine standard time for a task using stopwatch. Basis for wage payment.
Motion StudyEliminate unnecessary movements. Identify best method via Therbligs (17 basic motions by Gilbreth).
Fatigue StudyDetermine rest intervals to reduce fatigue and maintain productivity.
Differential Piece-Rate SystemHigh rate for workers meeting standard, low rate for those below. Incentivises performance.
Functional Foremanship8 specialists (4 planning + 4 production) instead of one foreman. Specialisation in supervision.
Functional Foremanship — 8 specialists:
Planning (4): Route Clerk, Instruction Card Clerk, Time & Cost Clerk, Disciplinarian
Production (4): Gang Boss, Speed Boss, Repair Boss, Inspector

Part 4 — Evolution of Management Thought

Classical → Neo-Classical → Modern → Contemporary

Classical School

TheoristTheoryKey Contribution
Henri Fayol (1916)Administrative/Classical Theory14 Principles, 5 Functions (POCCC)
F.W. Taylor (1911)Scientific ManagementTime-motion study, Functional foremanship, Diff. piece rate
Max Weber (1920s)Bureaucracy TheoryRational-legal authority, Formal rules, Hierarchy, Impersonality

Weber's Bureaucracy — 6 Features

  1. Division of Labour: Clearly defined roles and specialisation
  2. Hierarchy: Clear chain of command — each position supervised by higher one
  3. Formal Rules & Regulations: Written rules govern actions — consistency
  4. Impersonality: Rules apply equally to all — no personal favouritism
  5. Career Orientation: Employment based on technical qualifications, promotion by merit
  6. Official Records: All decisions in writing — administrative memory
Weber's 3 types of authority: Traditional (custom/tradition), Charismatic (personality), Rational-Legal (rules/laws) ← Bureaucracy uses this

4.2 Neo-Classical School — Hawthorne Studies

Elton Mayo & Hawthorne Studies (1924–1932)

  • Conducted at Hawthorne Works, Western Electric Company, Chicago
  • Led by Elton Mayo (Australian psychologist) with Roethlisberger & Dickson
  • Funded by National Research Council of USA
StudyWhat ChangedFinding
Illumination ExperimentsLight intensity (brighter/dimmer)Productivity rose regardless of light — attention itself motivated workers
Relay Assembly Test RoomRest periods, working hours, refreshmentsProductivity rose consistently — workers responded to being observed (Hawthorne Effect)
Mass Interviewing ProgrammeInterviews with 21,000 workers over 3 yrsComplaints revealed social/emotional factors more important than physical
Bank Wiring Observation RoomObservation of natural work groupInformal groups set own norms (restricting output); social control = powerful

Key Conclusions of Hawthorne Studies

  1. Workers are social beings — social needs matter, not just economic
  2. Informal groups are powerful and influence behaviour
  3. Being watched/special → increased productivity = Hawthorne Effect
  4. Job satisfaction depends on recognition and participation
  5. Communication and non-economic rewards are motivators

4.3 Modern School

Systems Approach — 4 Components

ComponentDescription
InputResources — men, material, money, machines, information
Process / TransformationConversion of inputs → outputs through management functions
OutputProducts, services, profits, employee satisfaction
FeedbackInformation about output fed back to improve inputs/process
  • Open System: Interacts with environment (all business organisations)
  • Closed System: No interaction with environment (theoretical)
  • Subsystems: Departments within organisation — interdependent
  • Synergy: Whole > sum of parts (2+2=5 in management)

Contingency Approach (Situational Approach)

  • No single best way to manage — it depends on the situation
  • Key theorists: Lawrence & Lorsch, Joan Woodward, Fiedler
  • Contingency variables: Environment, Technology, Organisational size, Individual differences
  • Managers must analyse the situation and adapt their approach accordingly
Exam shortcut: Systems approach = organisation as interconnected whole. Contingency = "it depends" on situation. Both reject one-size-fits-all.

McKinsey 7S Framework (Peters & Waterman, 1982)

Hard Elements (3)
  • Strategy — Plan to achieve competitive advantage
  • Structure — Organisational chart, hierarchy
  • Systems — Processes, IT systems, workflows
Soft Elements (4)
  • Shared Values — Core values/culture (centre of model)
  • Skills — Competencies of the organisation
  • Style — Leadership style
  • Staff — Human resources, employee capabilities
Shared Values is at the centre — it connects all other 6 elements. All elements must be aligned for organisational effectiveness.

Nudge Theory — Thaler & Sunstein (2008)

  • Book: "Nudge: Improving Decisions about Health, Wealth and Happiness" (2008)
  • Richard Thaler — Nobel Prize Economics 2017
  • A nudge = any aspect of choice architecture that alters behaviour without forbidding options or changing economic incentives
  • Based on behavioural economics — people are irrational but predictably so
  • Concept: Libertarian Paternalism — preserve freedom of choice while nudging toward better outcomes
  • Choice Architecture: Designing environments to influence decisions
Nudge examples:
• Cafeteria: placing healthy food at eye level (without banning junk food)
• Pension: auto-enroll employees (opt-out vs opt-in)
• Tax compliance: "9 out of 10 people pay tax on time" (social proof nudge)
• Organ donation: default opt-in country policies

Part 5 — Mintzberg's 10 Managerial Roles

Henry Mintzberg · 1973 · "The Nature of Managerial Work"
🔥 HIGH PROBABILITY — Frequently tested in Phase 1 MCQs and Phase 2

Mintzberg's roles appear in MCQs testing "which role does X action represent?" — learn all 10 with specific examples.

Background

  • Henry Mintzberg — Canadian academic, McGill University
  • Study: Observed 5 CEOs for 1 week each — identified actual activities
  • Challenged Fayol's POCC view — argued management is fragmented, action-oriented, not systematic planning
  • Published in "The Nature of Managerial Work" (1973)
Interpersonal (3)
1. Figurehead
Symbolic head — performs ceremonial/social duties. Signing documents, greeting visitors, attending functions.
Interpersonal (3)
2. Leader
Motivating, training, and directing subordinates. Hiring, firing, praising. Defines work atmosphere.
Interpersonal (3)
3. Liaison
Maintains network of external contacts. Industry associations, peers outside chain of command. Information exchange.
Informational (3)
4. Monitor
Seeks and receives information. Scans environment. Reads reports, attends briefings. Nerve centre of organisation.
Informational (3)
5. Disseminator
Transmits information to subordinates. Forwards memos, makes calls. Distributes what Monitor received.
Informational (3)
6. Spokesperson
Transmits information to external parties. Press releases, board presentations, lobbying. Official voice of organisation.
Decisional (4)
7. Entrepreneur
Initiates and designs change. New product development, process improvement. Seeks opportunities, takes initiative.
Decisional (4)
8. Disturbance Handler
Responds to involuntary crises. Strikes, supplier failure, competitor action. Reactive — forced into action.
Decisional (4)
9. Resource Allocator
Decides who gets what resources — money, time, equipment, people. Budgeting, scheduling, authorising decisions.
Decisional (4)
10. Negotiator
Represents organisation in major negotiations — labour contracts, client deals, acquisitions. Commits organisational resources.
MCQ traps:
• CEO giving a press conference = Spokesperson (not Figurehead — Figurehead is ceremonial, Spokesperson is informational)
• CEO handling a factory strike = Disturbance Handler
• CEO approving next year's budget = Resource Allocator
• CEO networking at an industry event = Liaison (not Figurehead)

Controlling — Process & Techniques

  1. Establish standards — set benchmarks (output, quality, time)
  2. Measure actual performance — quantitative/qualitative measurement
  3. Compare performance with standards — identify deviations
  4. Analyse deviations — find causes
  5. Take corrective action — remedial measures
Management by Exception (MBE): Managers focus only on significant deviations — not minor ones. Related to controlling.

Management by Objectives (MBO) — Peter Drucker (1954)

Book: "The Practice of Management" (1954)

  1. Set organisational goals — top management defines objectives
  2. Cascade to departments/individuals — align individual with organisational goals
  3. Employee participates in setting own goals — increases commitment
  4. Monitor and review progress periodically
  5. Evaluate performance against agreed objectives
  6. Reward/feedback — recognition and corrective action
Advantages of MBO
  • Clear goals → less ambiguity
  • Employee participation → motivation
  • Objective performance appraisal
  • Better communication
  • Aligns individual + org goals
Limitations of MBO
  • Time-consuming process
  • May create rigid goal orientation
  • Difficult to set quantifiable goals for all roles
  • Top management may not participate fully

Simon's Bounded Rationality (1947)

  • Herbert Simon — Nobel Prize Economics 1978
  • Decision-makers have bounded (limited) rationality — cannot process all information
  • Satisficing (not optimising) — choose first "good enough" option, not the best
  • Reasons for bounded rationality: cognitive limitations, time constraints, incomplete information
  • Contrasts with classical economic "rational man" assumption

Part 9 — Personality

Definition of Personality

  • Gordon Allport (1937): "Personality is the dynamic organisation within the individual of those psychophysical systems that determine his unique adjustments to his environment."
  • Personality = sum total of how an individual reacts and interacts with others
  • It is unique, consistent, and relatively stable over time

Allport's Trait Theory — 3 Types of Traits

Trait TypeDescriptionExample
Cardinal TraitsDominant, pervasive — define the person entirely. Very few people have a cardinal trait.Gandhi's non-violence; Hitler's authoritarianism
Central TraitsGeneral characteristics — describe personality. Most people have 5-10 central traits.Honest, aggressive, kind, intelligent
Secondary TraitsPeripheral, situational — appear in specific contexts only.Gets nervous during presentations; dislikes specific foods

Key Personality Attributes (Robbins)

AttributeDescription
Core Self-EvaluationSelf-assessment of capabilities, worth. Includes self-esteem, locus of control, neuroticism, self-efficacy. High = positive self-view = better job performance.
MachiavellianismHigh Mach = pragmatic, manipulative, emotionally detached. "End justifies means." Named after Niccolò Machiavelli.
NarcissismGrandiosity, entitlement, need for admiration. High narcissists are arrogant, self-promoting, poor team players.
Self-MonitoringAdjusting behaviour based on external cues. High SM = adaptable, good actors. Low SM = consistent regardless of situation.
Risk-TakingPropensity to take risks. High risk-takers: faster decisions, less information needed. Important for entrepreneurs.

Type A & Type B Personality — Friedman & Rosenman (1950s)

FeatureType AType B
PaceAlways in a hurry, time-pressuredRelaxed, unhurried
CompetitionHighly competitiveLess competitive
MultitaskingDoes multiple things simultaneouslyOne thing at a time
Work styleWorkaholic, aggressive, impatientDoes not need recognition, easy-going

Remember: Type A personalities make good managers in crisis but risk burnout. Type B are better for creative, long-term projects.

Factors Affecting Personality

Heredity / Genetics
  • Biological, physical, physiological makeup
  • Determined at conception
  • Eye colour, temperament, energy level
  • Twin studies support genetic influence
Environment
  • Family, culture, peer groups
  • Social norms and values
  • Socioeconomic class
  • Education and experiences
Life Experiences / Situation
  • Critical incidents shape personality
  • Trauma, success, failure
  • Different situations bring out different traits
Psychological Factors
  • Self-concept and self-awareness
  • Emotional intelligence
  • Cognitive patterns and beliefs
Nature vs Nurture debate: Both genetics and environment contribute. Modern view: genes set the range, environment determines where in that range we fall.

Part 10 — Big Five Personality Model (OCEAN)

McCrae & Costa · Most validated personality framework · 15-marker 2023

Background

  • Developed by McCrae & Costa (1990s), building on earlier work by Goldberg
  • Also called Five Factor Model (FFM)
  • Most empirically validated personality framework — used in hiring, leadership development, counselling
  • Trait-based — traits are continuous dimensions, not categories
Mnemonic: OCEAN — Open Conscientious Extraverted Agreeable Neurotic

Low O Conventional, routine-oriented, resistant to change, practical
Job fit: Research, design, art, entrepreneurship

Low C Disorganised, impulsive, unreliable, flexible, spontaneous
Best predictor of job performance across occupations
E

High E Sociable, talkative, assertive, energetic, seeks stimulation
Low E (Introverted) Reserved, quiet, prefers solitude, reflective, independent

RBI Grade B answer priority for Big Five:
1. Define each trait + give high/low characteristics
2. Mention Conscientiousness = best predictor of job performance
3. Neuroticism = emotional instability (inversely related to performance)
4. Link to HR decisions: hiring (C), team building (A+E), leadership (low N, high C, moderate O)

Part 11 — Perception

Definition · 7 Errors + Selective Perception

Definition of Perception

  • Perception = process by which individuals select, organise, and interpret sensory information to give meaning to their environment
  • Same stimulus → different perceptions → different behaviours
  • Influenced by: perceiver's characteristics, target's characteristics, situational context

Perception Errors — 7 Types + Selective Perception

#ErrorDefinitionExample
1Halo EffectGeneral impression from one positive trait applied to all qualities. "If good at X, must be good at everything."Hiring a candidate because they went to IIT, assuming excellence in all areas
2Horn EffectOpposite of Halo. One negative trait colours all judgments negatively.Candidate with poor grammar assumed to be incompetent overall
3StereotypingJudging someone based on group membership (gender, race, caste, region)."Women are not good at technical roles"
4Similarity (Like Me) EffectFavour people who are similar to ourselves in background, interests, or values.Manager promotes employee from same college
5Contrast EffectEvaluation affected by comparison with others encountered recently.Average candidate seems excellent after interviewing weak candidates
6IllusionFalse perception of real stimulus. Brain misinterprets actual external stimulus.Mirage in desert; straight stick appears bent in water
7HallucinationPerception without any real external stimulus. Internal experience mistaken as external.Seeing/hearing things that aren't there
+Selective PerceptionPeople selectively interpret based on interests, background, experience. Filter out information that conflicts with existing beliefs.Manager hears only positive feedback, ignores criticism
MCQ trap — Halo vs Horn:
Halo = one POSITIVE trait → positive overall impression
Horn = one NEGATIVE trait → negative overall impression
Contrast vs Selective: Contrast = comparison with others. Selective = filtering information based on own beliefs.

Part 12 — Practice MCQs (30 Questions)

Click to reveal answers · Cover options and answer first

Management Fundamentals (Q1–Q10)

Q1. According to Fayol, which principle states that an employee should receive orders from only one superior? (a) Unity of Direction (b) Unity of Command (c) Scalar Chain (d) Esprit de Corps
Answer: (b) Unity of Command
Unity of Command = one boss per employee. Unity of Direction = one plan per group of activities. Common trap: students confuse these two.
Q2. "Gang Plank" is associated with which of Fayol's principles? (a) Unity of Command (b) Scalar Chain (c) Discipline (d) Division of Work
Answer: (b) Scalar Chain
Gang Plank = horizontal communication between same-level managers when urgency requires bypassing the scalar chain. It's an exception Fayol allowed within the Scalar Chain principle.
Q3. Which management theory is associated with "Hawthorne Effect"? (a) Classical Theory (b) Scientific Management (c) Human Relations Theory (d) Systems Theory
Answer: (c) Human Relations Theory
Elton Mayo's Hawthorne Studies at Western Electric led to Neo-Classical / Human Relations Theory. Finding: being observed increases productivity (Hawthorne Effect).
Q4. POSDCORB was given by: (a) Henry Fayol (b) F.W. Taylor (c) Luther Gulick (d) Koontz & O'Donnell
Answer: (c) Luther Gulick
Gulick = POSDCORB (Planning, Organising, Staffing, Directing, Coordinating, Reporting, Budgeting). Fayol = POCCC. Koontz = POSDC.
Q5. Theory Z was developed by: (a) Douglas McGregor (b) William Ouchi (c) Abraham Maslow (d) Frederick Herzberg
Answer: (b) William Ouchi
Ouchi (1981) combined American (Theory A) + Japanese (Theory J) management to create Theory Z organisation. McGregor = Theory X and Theory Y.
Q6. Nudge Theory was proposed by: (a) Peter Drucker (b) Herbert Simon (c) Richard Thaler & Cass Sunstein (d) William Ouchi
Answer: (c) Richard Thaler & Cass Sunstein
Thaler & Sunstein published "Nudge" in 2008. Thaler won Nobel Prize in Economics 2017 for contributions to behavioural economics including nudge theory.
Q7. Which of the following is a HARD element in McKinsey 7S framework? (a) Skills (b) Style (c) Strategy (d) Shared Values
Answer: (c) Strategy
Hard elements (3S): Strategy, Structure, Systems. Soft elements (4S): Shared Values, Skills, Style, Staff. Skills, Style, and Shared Values are all SOFT elements.
Q8. Which principle of Fayol states that "a place for everything and everything in its place"? (a) Equity (b) Initiative (c) Order (d) Unity of Direction
Answer: (c) Order
Order has two parts: Material Order (physical things in right place) and Social Order (right person in right job). The quote refers to Material Order.
Q9. Taylor's technique involving 8 specialists instead of one foreman is called: (a) Time Study (b) Differential Piece Rate (c) Functional Foremanship (d) Motion Study
Answer: (c) Functional Foremanship
Taylor replaced one all-knowing foreman with 8 specialists (4 planning + 4 production). This violated Fayol's Unity of Command but increased specialisation.
Q10. Which approach to management says "there is no single best way to manage"? (a) Classical approach (b) Systems approach (c) Contingency approach (d) Behavioural approach
Answer: (c) Contingency approach
Contingency = "it depends on the situation." Lawrence & Lorsch, Joan Woodward are key contributors. No universal principles — management style must adapt to environment, technology, and people.

Mintzberg & Decision Making (Q11–Q18)

Q11. A CEO handling a major labour strike falls under which Mintzberg role? (a) Entrepreneur (b) Negotiator (c) Disturbance Handler (d) Resource Allocator
Answer: (c) Disturbance Handler
Disturbance Handler = reactive role dealing with involuntary crises (strikes, supplier failures, competitor actions). Negotiator = planned negotiations. Entrepreneur = proactive change-making.
Q12. A CEO giving a press conference to announce quarterly results is performing which role? (a) Figurehead (b) Liaison (c) Spokesperson (d) Disseminator
Answer: (c) Spokesperson
Spokesperson = transmits information to EXTERNAL parties. Figurehead = ceremonial/symbolic. Disseminator = transmits info to INTERNAL subordinates. Liaison = external networking (not formal communication).
Q13. In the Delphi technique, which of the following is TRUE? (a) Members meet face-to-face (b) Ideas are generated without criticism in rounds (c) Experts respond to questionnaires anonymously (d) Inner group discusses while outer group observes
Answer: (c) Experts respond to questionnaires anonymously
Delphi = remote, anonymous, iterative questionnaires until consensus. (a) = wrong — no face-to-face. (b) = Brainstorming. (d) = Fish Bowl technique.
Q14. In Nominal Group Technique (NGT), what happens FIRST? (a) Group votes on ideas (b) Facilitator presents problem and members write ideas independently (c) Open group discussion (d) Ideas are criticised and evaluated
Answer: (b) Facilitator presents problem and members write ideas independently
NGT sequence: problem presented → individual silent writing → round-robin sharing → group discussion → voting/ranking. The silent individual writing phase is what distinguishes NGT from brainstorming.
Q15. Herbert Simon's concept of "satisficing" is associated with: (a) Classical economic rationality (b) Bounded rationality (c) Scientific management (d) Contingency theory
Answer: (b) Bounded rationality
Simon (Nobel 1978) argued decision-makers have bounded (limited) rationality due to cognitive limits and incomplete information. Therefore they "satisfice" (choose first good-enough option) rather than optimise.
Q16. PERT differs from CPM in that PERT: (a) Identifies the critical path (b) Uses deterministic time estimates (c) Uses three time estimates for uncertain activities (d) Is used in construction projects
Answer: (c) Uses three time estimates for uncertain activities
PERT uses Optimistic (O), Most Likely (M), and Pessimistic (P) time estimates → weighted average. Used in R&D/uncertain projects. CPM uses one deterministic estimate, used in construction.
Q17. Quality Circles involve: (a) Top management only (b) External consultants solving quality problems (c) Small groups of workers who voluntarily meet to discuss quality (d) Structured debate between two groups
Answer: (c) Small groups of workers who voluntarily meet to discuss quality
Quality Circles = voluntary groups of 8-10 workers at operational level who identify and solve quality problems. Japanese origin. (d) = Didactic Interaction.
Q18. MBO (Management by Objectives) was introduced by: (a) Elton Mayo (b) Henry Fayol (c) Peter Drucker (d) Douglas McGregor
Answer: (c) Peter Drucker
Drucker introduced MBO in "The Practice of Management" (1954). Key idea: individual and organisational objectives must be aligned; employees participate in setting their own goals.

Personality & Perception (Q19–Q30)

Q19. Among the Big Five personality traits, which is considered the BEST predictor of job performance? (a) Extraversion (b) Openness (c) Conscientiousness (d) Agreeableness
Answer: (c) Conscientiousness
Conscientiousness (organised, disciplined, reliable) is the strongest and most consistent predictor of job performance across all occupations. High-C individuals achieve more, are more punctual, and follow through on commitments.
Q20. Definition of personality was given by Gordon Allport in: (a) 1927 (b) 1937 (c) 1947 (d) 1957
Answer: (b) 1937
Gordon Allport defined personality in his 1937 work "Personality: A Psychological Interpretation." His definition: "dynamic organisation within the individual of those psychophysical systems that determine his unique adjustments to his environment."
Q21. Type A and Type B personality types were identified by: (a) Allport & Odbert (b) McCrae & Costa (c) Friedman & Rosenman (d) Maslow & Herzberg
Answer: (c) Friedman & Rosenman
Cardiologists Meyer Friedman and Ray Rosenman identified Type A/B personality in the 1950s while studying heart disease risk factors. Type A = time-pressured, competitive, higher heart disease risk.
Q22. A manager who gives high performance ratings to a candidate simply because they attended a prestigious university is exhibiting: (a) Stereotyping (b) Halo Effect (c) Contrast Effect (d) Horn Effect
Answer: (b) Halo Effect
Halo Effect = one positive characteristic (prestigious university) creates an overall positive impression applied to unrelated traits. Stereotyping would be judging based on group membership (e.g., IITians are always good at maths).
Q23. "Seeing things that don't exist in reality" is: (a) Illusion (b) Halo Effect (c) Hallucination (d) Selective Perception
Answer: (c) Hallucination
Hallucination = perception without real external stimulus (internal). Illusion = misinterpretation of real external stimulus (e.g., mirage = real heat waves but brain interprets as water). Key difference: hallucination has NO real stimulus; illusion has a real but misinterpreted stimulus.
Q24. Allport's Cardinal Traits are BEST described as: (a) Common traits seen in all people (b) Situational traits appearing in specific contexts (c) Dominant traits that define the entire personality (d) Traits specific to workplace behaviour
Answer: (c) Dominant traits that define the entire personality
Cardinal traits are the most pervasive — they define the whole person. Few people have them. Examples: Gandhi's non-violence, Hitler's authoritarianism, Mother Teresa's compassion. Central traits = 5-10 characteristics most people have. Secondary = situational.
Q25. Which Big Five trait is associated with being ANXIOUS, MOODY, and EMOTIONALLY UNSTABLE? (a) Low Extraversion (b) Low Agreeableness (c) High Neuroticism (d) Low Openness
Answer: (c) High Neuroticism
High Neuroticism = emotional instability, anxiety, moodiness. Also called "emotional instability" — some models score it reversed as "Emotional Stability." High N is associated with lower job performance and higher stress.
Q26. The Big Five model is also called: (a) DISC model (b) Five Factor Model (FFM) (c) Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (d) HEXACO model
Answer: (b) Five Factor Model (FFM)
Big Five = Five Factor Model (FFM). Developed by McCrae & Costa. MBTI = Myers-Briggs (different framework, 16 types). DISC = different assessment. HEXACO = extended version with 6 factors.
Q27. An interviewer is more impressed by a mediocre candidate after interviewing several very poor candidates. This is: (a) Halo Effect (b) Stereotyping (c) Contrast Effect (d) Selective Perception
Answer: (c) Contrast Effect
Contrast Effect = evaluation influenced by comparison with recently encountered others. The mediocre candidate isn't objectively good — they only seem better in CONTRAST to worse candidates. Classic interview bias.
Q28. A high Machiavellian person is BEST described as: (a) Insecure and emotionally volatile (b) Pragmatic, manipulative, and believing ends justify means (c) Overly cooperative and trusting (d) Resistant to change and conventional
Answer: (b) Pragmatic, manipulative, and believing ends justify means
Named after Niccolò Machiavelli's "The Prince." High Mach = manipulative, detached, believes in doing whatever it takes to achieve goals. Succeed in jobs requiring bargaining/persuasion. (a) = High Neuroticism. (c) = High Agreeableness. (d) = Low Openness.
Q29. Hawthorne Studies were conducted at: (a) Ford Motor Company (b) Midvale Steel Company (c) Western Electric Company (d) Bethlehem Steel
Answer: (c) Western Electric Company
Hawthorne Works, Western Electric Company, Chicago (1924-1932). Led by Elton Mayo. Midvale Steel = where Taylor worked. Bethlehem Steel = where Taylor conducted his pig iron experiments.
Q30. Which Fayol principle was VIOLATED by Taylor's Functional Foremanship? (a) Unity of Direction (b) Scalar Chain (c) Unity of Command (d) Division of Work
Answer: (c) Unity of Command
Unity of Command = one boss per employee. Taylor's Functional Foremanship gave each worker 8 bosses (specialists). This directly violates Unity of Command. This is one of the key criticisms of Taylor's approach from a Fayolian perspective.

15-Marker Template — Fayol's 14 Principles (2023 PYQ)
Introduction (30–40 words)

Henri Fayol, widely regarded as the Father of Modern Management, propounded 14 universal principles of management in his seminal work "Administration Industrielle et Générale" (1916). These principles provide a comprehensive framework for effective organisational management.

Body — 14 Principles (list format with brief explanation) (~200 words)

Fayol identified the following 14 principles:
1. Division of Work — Specialisation improves efficiency...
2. Authority & Responsibility — Equal and commensurate...
[Continue through all 14 with one-line explanations each]
[For 15 marks, elaborate on at least 5-6 key ones: Unity of Command, Scalar Chain, Equity, Esprit de Corps, Centralisation]

Relevance to Modern Management (80–100 words)

Despite being formulated over a century ago, Fayol's principles remain highly relevant. Unity of Command aligns with modern matrix organisation debates. Centralisation vs decentralisation remains a strategic choice in flat vs hierarchical organisations. Esprit de Corps underpins modern team culture and employee engagement. Equity and Stability of Tenure inform contemporary HR retention strategies. However, rigid adherence to scalar chain has been replaced by agile communication networks. The principles serve as guidelines, not absolute laws — reflecting Fayol's own view that management is an art as much as a science.

Conclusion (20–30 words)

Fayol's 14 principles constitute a foundational framework that continues to guide management practice globally, offering both structure and flexibility for diverse organisational contexts.

15-Marker Template — Big Five Personality Model (2023 PYQ)
Introduction (30–40 words)

The Big Five Personality Model, also known as the Five Factor Model (FFM), developed by McCrae and Costa, is the most empirically validated framework for understanding human personality. It identifies five broad dimensions that comprehensively capture personality variation across individuals.

The Five Traits — OCEAN Acronym (150 words)

1. Openness to Experience: Curiosity, creativity, willingness to try new things. High O = innovative; Low O = conventional.
2. Conscientiousness: Organisation, reliability, goal-directedness. Best predictor of job performance across occupations.
3. Extraversion: Sociability, assertiveness, positive emotions. High E = suited for leadership/sales roles.
4. Agreeableness: Cooperativeness, trust, empathy. High A = team players; suited for HR, customer service.
5. Neuroticism: Emotional instability, anxiety, moodiness. High N → lower performance; low N (emotional stability) desirable in high-stress roles.

Organisational Applications (100 words)

The Big Five has significant applications in HRM. During selection: High C candidates are preferred for most roles; high A for customer-facing roles; high E for leadership positions. In team building: balanced A+E creates collaborative teams while low N ensures stability. For leadership development: high C and low N with moderate O characterise effective leaders. In performance management: understanding personality helps tailor motivation and feedback styles. However, ethical considerations mandate that personality testing be one input among several, avoiding over-reliance or potential discrimination.

Critical Evaluation + Conclusion (50 words)

While the Big Five has cross-cultural validity and strong predictive power, critics note it describes rather than explains behaviour, and situational factors also significantly influence outcomes. Nevertheless, it remains the gold standard for personality assessment in organisational contexts, offering actionable insights for strategic HRM decisions.

Target: 400–450 words | Time: 20 minutes | Remember: Quote McCrae & Costa + FFM + Conscientiousness = best predictor
15-Marker Template — Trait Theory vs Behavioural Theory of Leadership (2024 PYQ)
Introduction (30 words)

Leadership theories have evolved from early trait-based approaches to behavioural models. Both offer valuable but contrasting perspectives on what makes an effective leader.

Trait Theory (100 words)

Trait Theory (Great Man Theory) holds that leaders are BORN with innate qualities. Key traits identified: intelligence, confidence, integrity, charisma, emotional stability, dominance. Stogdill (1948, 1974) conducted landmark reviews of leadership traits. Limitations: no universal trait set; ignores situational factors; can be used to discriminate. Gordon Allport's trait hierarchy (Cardinal/Central/Secondary) provides the personality backdrop.

Behavioural Theory (100 words)

Behavioural theories hold that leadership can be LEARNED through observed behaviours. Key studies: Ohio State Studies (Initiating Structure vs Consideration), Michigan Studies (Production-centred vs Employee-centred), Blake & Mouton Managerial Grid (5 leadership styles: Impoverished, Authority-Compliance, Country Club, Team, Middle-of-the-road). Advantage: trainable. Limitation: ignores situational context — same style does not work everywhere.

Comparison Table + Conclusion (60 words)

Both theories have limitations leading to situational/contingency theories (Fiedler, Hersey & Blanchard). Modern leadership integrates traits, behaviour, and situational sensitivity. An effective RBI Grade B officer must demonstrate analytical traits while adapting communication style (behaviour) to team and context.

Target: 400 words | Key names: Stogdill, Ohio State, Michigan Studies, Blake & Mouton
