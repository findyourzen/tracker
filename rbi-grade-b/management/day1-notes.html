Part 1 — Introduction to Management

Definitions · Levels · Admin vs Management · Nature
Must Know
PYQ: RBI Phase 2 2022 — 'Is management a science or an art?'
Plain English
A school needs someone to decide WHAT subjects to teach (administration) and someone to make teachers actually teach them well (management). Fayol said management means getting your team to plan, organise, command, coordinate and control. Koontz said it's simpler: get things done through other people.
KEY DEFINITIONS — Quote these in answers
Koontz & O'Donnell ← Most cited in RBI
"Getting things done through and with people in formally organised groups"
Henri Fayol
"To manage is to forecast and plan, to organise, to command, to coordinate and to control"
Mary Parker Follett (earliest)
"Art of getting things done through people"
Peter Drucker (two quotes)
"Management is doing things right; Leadership is doing the right things" · Also: "Multi-purpose organ managing business, managers and work"
F.W. Taylor
"Art of knowing what you want to do and then seeing that they do it in the best and cheapest way"
Management as Science
  • Systematised body of knowledge
  • Universal principles (Fayol's principles)
  • Cause-and-effect relationships
  • Tested through observation
Management as Art
  • Personalised application of knowledge
  • Requires creativity & judgment
  • Improves with practice
  • Goal-oriented, result-focused
Exam answer: Management is BOTH science AND art — an "inexact science." Principles are guidelines not rigid laws. Always mention both sides.

Part 2 — Fayol's 14 Principles of Management

Classical Theory · Father of Modern Management · Most tested section
Must Know15-Marker 2023
PYQ: RBI Phase 2 2023 (15 marks) — Know ALL 14 cold with examples
Plain English
Fayol ran a massive mining company in France. He wrote down the rules he used to manage it, so ANY manager could use them. Think of his 14 principles as the original management rulebook — written in 1916, still taught everywhere. The two most confused: Unity of Command (one boss per person) vs Unity of Direction (one plan per project).
FAYOL — Background
French industrialist · Comambault Mining Company · Rose to Director General
Published "Administration Industrielle et Générale" [1916] · Father of Modern Management Theory
Focus: ENTIRE organisation (top-down)
vs Taylor's shop floor focus · Universal — applies to ALL types of organisations
6 activities of industrial firms
Technical · Commercial · Financial · Security · Accounting · Managerial
Mnemonic for 14 Principles
DAD · UUS · R · CSO · E · SIE
Division · Authority · Discipline · Unity of Command · Unity of Direction · Subordination · Remuneration · Centralisation · Scalar Chain · Order · Equity · Stability · Initiative · Esprit de Corps
Principle 1
Division of Work
Specialisation → efficiency. Each person focuses on one task. Applies to technical AND managerial work.
Ex: Assembly line — each worker does one task only
Principle 2
Authority & Responsibility
Authority = right to give orders. Responsibility = obligation to perform. Must be EQUAL. Imbalance causes problems.
Ex: If manager can hire, must be accountable for team output
Principle 3
Discipline
Obedience, application, energy, behaviour. Requires good supervision, fair agreements, judicious penalties.
Ex: Attendance rules, code of conduct
Principle 4
Unity of Command
Each employee receives orders from ONE superior only. Multiple bosses = confusion, conflict, undermined authority. Individual level.
Ex: No employee reports to two managers simultaneously
Principle 5
Unity of Direction
ONE head, ONE plan for a group of activities with the SAME objective. About the organisation, not the individual.
Ex: All marketing activities under one plan and one head
Principle 6
Subordination of Individual Interest
Individual/group interests subordinated to organisational interest. Org's interest is SUPREME.
Ex: Employee skips personal plans for critical project deadline
Principle 7
Remuneration
Fair compensation satisfying BOTH employee and employer. Avoid over/underpayment. Methods: time rate, job rate, profit sharing.
Ex: Performance-linked pay ensuring motivation
Principle 8
Centralisation
No absolute rule — optimal balance depends on org size. Startup = high centralisation; Large MNC = decentralised.
Ex: Startup founder decides everything; MNC has region heads
Principle 9
Scalar Chain + Gang Plank
Chain of authority from highest to lowest. Communication flows along this. Gang Plank = horizontal communication between SAME-LEVEL managers in emergencies (Fayol allowed this exception).
Ex: Org chart hierarchy; direct peer contact in urgency
Principle 10
Order
Material Order: a place for everything, everything in its place. Social Order: right person in the right place.
Ex: Filing system; job descriptions matching skills
Principle 11
Equity
Combination of kindness and justice. Equal and fair treatment for all employees. Promotes loyalty and devotion.
Ex: No discrimination by gender/caste in promotions
Principle 12
Stability of Tenure
High turnover is harmful. Time needed to settle into job. Avoid frequent transfers. Mediocre stable manager > brilliant unstable one.
Ex: Job security policies, low attrition culture
Principle 13
Initiative
Freedom to conceive and execute plans within limits of authority. Must be encouraged — enhances zeal and energy.
Ex: Google's 20% personal project time policy
Principle 14
Esprit de Corps
Team spirit. Union is strength. Avoid divide-and-rule and excessive written communication over verbal.
Ex: Team celebrations, collaborative culture
Most Confused Pair — Unity of Command vs Unity of Direction:
Unity of Command = one boss per EMPLOYEE → individual level
Unity of Direction = one plan per ACTIVITY GROUP → organisational level
Exam trap: "Marketing team under one marketing plan" = Unity of Direction (NOT Command)

Part 3 — Taylor's Scientific Management

Classical Theory · Father of Scientific Management · Shop-floor focus
Must Know
PYQ: Regularly tested — compare with Fayol · Functional Foremanship MCQs
Plain English
Taylor was an engineer at a steel factory who was frustrated watching workers go slow deliberately. He said: replace guesswork with science. Time every task with a stopwatch. Find the single best way to do it. Train workers for that exact method. Pay more for meeting the target, less for missing it. His approach was great for factories, but felt dehumanising — workers were treated like machines.
TAYLOR — Background
American mechanical engineer · Midvale Steel → Bethlehem Steel
Published "Principles of Scientific Management" [1911] · Father of Scientific Management
Problem he was solving: "Soldiering"
Workers deliberately slowed down output · Taylor called this "natural soldiering" (laziness) + "systematic soldiering" (group norms to restrict output)
Focus: Shop floor / Workers (bottom-up) vs Fayol's top-down
Developed Time and Motion Study to find the 'one best way'
Taylor's 5 Principles
S · S · C · S · M
Science not Rule of Thumb · Scientific Selection & Training · Cooperation not Discord · Scientific Division of Work · Maximum Output
TAYLOR'S 5 TECHNIQUES
Time Study
Determine standard time for a task using stopwatch · Basis for wage payment
Motion Study — Therbligs
Eliminate unnecessary movements · Therbligs = 17 basic motions identified by Gilbreth · Find ONE best method
Fatigue Study
Determine rest intervals to reduce fatigue and maintain sustained productivity
Differential Piece-Rate System
High rate for workers meeting/exceeding standard · Low rate for those below · Incentivises performance
Functional Foremanship — 8 specialists instead of 1 foreman
Planning (4):
Route Clerk · Instruction Card Clerk · Time & Cost Clerk · Disciplinarian
Production (4):
Gang Boss · Speed Boss · Repair Boss · Inspector
Violates Fayol's Unity of Command — 8 bosses per worker

Fayol vs Taylor — Key Comparison

Henri Fayol
  • Whole organisation (top-down)
  • Manager / Administrator background
  • Father of Modern Management
  • General theory — universal applicability
  • Published 1916 (French)
  • Respected Unity of Command
F.W. Taylor
  • Shop floor / workers (bottom-up)
  • Engineer / Technician background
  • Father of Scientific Management
  • Manufacturing / production settings
  • Published 1911 (English)
  • VIOLATED Unity of Command (8 bosses)

Part 4 — Evolution of Management Thought

Classical → Neo-Classical → Modern → Contemporary
Must Know
PYQ: Hawthorne Effect MCQs appear every year · Theory Z distinguish from McGregor
Plain English
Management thinking went through 4 stages: First people cared only about rules and structure (Classical — Fayol/Taylor/Weber). Then they discovered workers have feelings and social needs (Neo-Classical — Mayo). Then they saw the org as an interconnected system (Modern — Systems). Then they said "it depends" on the situation (Contingency). Today we use nudges to change behaviour without commands (Contemporary).

4.1 Classical School

Fayol [1916]
Administrative Theory
14 Principles · 5 Functions (POCCC) · Top-down
Taylor [1911]
Scientific Management
Time-motion study · Diff. piece rate · Functional foremanship
Weber [1920s]
Bureaucracy Theory
Rational-legal authority · Formal rules · Hierarchy · Impersonality
WEBER'S BUREAUCRACY — 6 Features
① Division of Labour
Clearly defined roles and specialisation — everyone knows their job
② Hierarchy
Clear chain of command — each position supervised by higher one
③ Formal Rules & Regulations
Written rules govern all actions — consistency and predictability
④ Impersonality
Rules apply equally to ALL — no personal favouritism
⑤ Career Orientation
Employment based on technical qualifications · Promotion by merit (not connections)
⑥ Official Records
All decisions in writing — administrative memory maintained
Weber's 3 Authority Types
Traditional (custom/tradition) · Charismatic (personality) · Rational-Legal (rules/laws) ← Bureaucracy uses this

4.2 Neo-Classical School — Hawthorne Studies

HAWTHORNE STUDIES [1924–1932] — Elton Mayo
Where: Hawthorne Works, Western Electric Company, Chicago
Led by Elton Mayo (Australian psychologist) + Roethlisberger & Dickson · Funded by National Research Council, USA
① Illumination Experiments
Changed light intensity (brighter/dimmer) → Productivity rose regardless of light level · Finding: attention itself motivated workers
② Relay Assembly Test Room
Changed rest periods, working hours, refreshments → Productivity kept rising · Workers responded to being observed = Hawthorne Effect
③ Mass Interviewing Programme (21,000 workers, 3 yrs)
Complaints revealed social/emotional factors more important than physical conditions
④ Bank Wiring Observation Room
Observed natural work group → Informal groups set own output norms (restricting production) · Social control = very powerful
5 KEY CONCLUSIONS — Hawthorne
Workers are social beings — social needs matter
Not just economic/money-driven · Relationships, belonging, recognition count
Informal groups are powerful
Can override management directives · Set their own norms of output
Hawthorne Effect: being watched → increased productivity
Workers perform better when they know they're being observed or feel they're special
Job satisfaction = recognition + participation
Being listened to matters more than physical improvements
Non-economic rewards motivate
Communication, praise, group belonging = powerful motivators beyond money

4.3 Modern School

SYSTEMS APPROACH — Organisation as Open System
Input
Resources: men, material, money, machines, information
Process / Transformation
Conversion of inputs to outputs through management functions
Output
Products, services, profits, employee satisfaction
Feedback loop
Information about output fed back to improve inputs/process
Open vs Closed System
Open = interacts with environment (all real orgs) · Closed = no interaction (theoretical only)
Synergy
Whole > sum of parts. 2+2=5 in management. Interdependent subsystems create more value together.
CONTINGENCY APPROACH — "It Depends"
Core idea: No single best way to manage
Depends on: environment · technology · org size · individual differences
Key theorists
Lawrence & Lorsch · Joan Woodward · Fiedler
Managers must analyse situation → adapt approach accordingly
Rejects one-size-fits-all. Also called Situational Approach.

4.4 Contemporary Theories

McKINSEY 7S FRAMEWORK — Peters & Waterman [1982]
Hard Elements (3) — tangible, easy to identify
Strategy (plan for competitive advantage) · Structure (org chart, hierarchy) · Systems (processes, IT, workflows)
Soft Elements (4) — culture, people
Shared Values · Skills · Style · Staff
Shared Values at the CENTRE
Connects all other 6 elements. All 7 must be aligned for organisational effectiveness.
NUDGE THEORY — Thaler & Sunstein [2008]
Book: "Nudge" (2008) · Thaler = Nobel Prize Economics 2017
Based on behavioural economics — people are irrational but predictably so
Nudge = alter behaviour WITHOUT banning options or changing incentives
Libertarian Paternalism: preserve freedom while nudging toward better outcomes · Choice Architecture: design environment to influence decisions
Examples
Cafeteria: healthy food at eye level · Pension: auto-enrol (opt-out) · Tax: "9/10 people pay on time" · Organ donation: default opt-in countries

Part 5 — Mintzberg's 10 Managerial Roles

Henry Mintzberg · 1973 · "The Nature of Managerial Work"
Must KnowHigh MCQ Frequency
PYQ: Phase 1 MCQs every year — 'Which role does CEO do X?' — Learn all 10 with specific examples
Plain English
Fayol said managers plan, organise, command, coordinate and control. Mintzberg watched ACTUAL CEOs for a week each and said: "that's not what they really do." Real managers jump between 10 different roles all day — sometimes a symbolic figurehead (cutting ribbons), sometimes a firefighter (handling crises), sometimes a budget setter, sometimes a spokesperson to the press.
Mintzberg — Background
Canadian academic, McGill University
Observed 5 CEOs for 1 week each · Published "The Nature of Managerial Work" [1973]
Challenged Fayol's POCCC view
Argued management is fragmented, action-oriented, reactive — NOT systematic planning
Interpersonal (1/3)
1. Figurehead
Symbolic/ceremonial head. Signs documents, greets visitors, attends official functions. No real decision power.
Interpersonal (2/3)
2. Leader
Motivates, trains, directs subordinates. Hiring, firing, praising. Defines work atmosphere and culture.
Interpersonal (3/3)
3. Liaison
Maintains network of EXTERNAL contacts outside chain of command — industry bodies, peers. Information exchange.
Informational (1/3)
4. Monitor
Seeks and receives information. Scans environment. Reads reports, attends briefings. Nerve centre of organisation.
Informational (2/3)
5. Disseminator
Transmits information INTERNALLY to subordinates. Forwards memos, makes internal calls. Distributes what Monitor received.
Informational (3/3)
6. Spokesperson
Transmits information to EXTERNAL parties. Press releases, board presentations, lobbying. Official voice of org.
Decisional (1/4)
7. Entrepreneur
Initiates and designs VOLUNTARY change. New products, process improvements. Seeks opportunities proactively.
Decisional (2/4)
8. Disturbance Handler
Responds to INVOLUNTARY crises. Strikes, supplier failure, competitor action. Reactive — forced into action.
Decisional (3/4)
9. Resource Allocator
Decides who gets what — money, time, equipment, people. Budgeting, scheduling, authorising decisions.
Decisional (4/4)
10. Negotiator
Represents org in major negotiations — labour contracts, client deals, acquisitions. Commits organisational resources.
MCQ Traps — These are tested every year:
CEO giving press conference on results = Spokesperson (NOT Figurehead — Figurehead is ceremonial only)
CEO networking at industry event = Liaison (NOT Figurehead — Liaison = external information network)
CEO handling factory strike = Disturbance Handler (involuntary, NOT Negotiator which is planned)
CEO approving next year's budget = Resource Allocator (NOT Entrepreneur)
CEO launching new product initiative = Entrepreneur (voluntary/proactive change)

Part 6 — Functions of Management

POCCC · POSDCORB · POSDC · Controlling
Must Know
PYQ: 'Who gave POSDCORB?' is a classic MCQ
3 CLASSIFICATION SYSTEMS — Know all three
Henri Fayol → POCCC (5 functions)
Planning · Organising · Commanding · Coordinating · Controlling
Luther Gulick → POSDCORB (7 functions) — most tested
Planning · Organising · Staffing · Directing · Coordinating · Reporting · Budgeting
Koontz & O'Donnell → POSDC (5 functions) — most accepted modern
Planning · Organising · Staffing · Directing · Controlling
CONTROLLING — 5-Step Process
① Establish standards
Set benchmarks — output, quality, time
② Measure actual performance
Quantitative or qualitative measurement
③ Compare performance with standards
Identify deviations — favourable and unfavourable
④ Analyse deviations
Find root causes of variance
⑤ Take corrective action
Remedial measures to get back on track
Management by Exception (MBE): Managers focus ONLY on significant deviations — not minor ones. Save time by ignoring the noise.

Part 7 — Planning & MBO

Types of Plans · MBO · Simon's Bounded Rationality
TYPES OF PLANS
By Time Horizon
Strategic Plans (3-5+ yrs)
Top management · Org-wide direction
Tactical Plans (1-3 yrs)
Middle management · Implement strategic plans
Operational Plans (<1 yr)
Lower management · Day-to-day work
By Use
Standing Plans (repeated use): Policies, Procedures, Rules, Methods
Single-use Plans (one-time): Budgets, Programmes, Projects
By Scope
Directional = flexible guidelines | Specific = clearly defined objectives, no ambiguity
MBO — Management by Objectives · Peter Drucker [1954]
Book: "The Practice of Management" (1954)
Joint goal-setting between managers and employees · Aligns individual objectives with org objectives
6-Step Process
① Set organisational goals (top mgmt)
② Cascade to depts/individuals
③ Employee participates in setting own goals
④ Monitor & review progress periodically
⑤ Evaluate performance vs agreed objectives
⑥ Reward / feedback
MBO Advantages
  • Clear goals → less ambiguity
  • Employee participation → motivation
  • Objective performance appraisal
  • Better communication
  • Aligns individual + org goals
MBO Limitations
  • Time-consuming process
  • Rigid goal orientation
  • Hard to quantify all roles
  • Top management may not participate
SIMON'S BOUNDED RATIONALITY [1947]
Herbert Simon — Nobel Prize Economics 1978
Decision-makers have LIMITED (bounded) rationality — cannot process all information
"Satisficing" not Optimising
Choose the first good-enough option rather than the single best option · Contrasts with classical "rational man" assumption
3 reasons for bounded rationality
Cognitive limitations · Time constraints · Incomplete information

Part 8 — Decision Making Techniques

Group Techniques · Quantitative Techniques · PERT vs CPM
Plain English
When groups need to make decisions there are different techniques — some need everyone together (brainstorming), some work remotely without face-to-face (Delphi), some are structured (NGT). For project planning, PERT handles uncertain time estimates; CPM handles certain ones.
GROUP DECISION-MAKING TECHNIQUES
Brainstorming
Free flow of ideas · No criticism during idea generation · Quantity over quality first · Then filter · Face-to-face
Delphi Technique
Anonymous questionnaires in rounds · No face-to-face · Experts geographically spread · Iterate until consensus
Nominal Group Technique (NGT)
Silent individual writing FIRST → round-robin sharing → group discussion → voting · Face-to-face but structured
Quality Circles
Voluntary groups of 8-10 workers meeting to solve quality problems · Worker-level, bottom-up · Japanese origin
Fish Bowl
Inner group discusses while outer observes · Roles rotate · Encourages active listening
Didactic Interaction
Two groups: one proposes, one opposes · Roles reversed · Structured devil's advocate — forces pros AND cons
PERT — Uncertain Projects
Programme Evaluation Review Technique
3 time estimates: Optimistic (O) · Most Likely (M) · Pessimistic (P)
Used in: R&D, new product development
Identifies critical path
CPM — Certain Projects
Critical Path Method
1 deterministic time estimate
Used in: Construction, repetitive projects
Also identifies critical path
PERT vs CPM trap: Both identify the critical path. Difference = PERT uses 3 estimates (uncertain) · CPM uses 1 estimate (certain) · PERT=R&D · CPM=construction

Part 9 — Personality

Allport · Trait Types · Type A/B · Robbins Attributes
Must Know15-Marker 2023
PYQ: Big Five Personality (15 marks, 2023) · Type A/B and Locus of Control MCQs
Plain English
Your personality is the pattern of how you think, feel and behave — your consistent, unique way of engaging with the world. Allport said most of us have 5-10 central traits that define us. A handful of rare people (Gandhi, Hitler) have one cardinal trait so powerful it defines EVERYTHING about them. Your boss who never stops working and has heart problems? Probably Type A.
ALLPORT'S TRAIT THEORY [1937]
Cardinal Traits — very rare
Dominant, pervasive trait that defines the ENTIRE person · Very few people have one
Ex: Gandhi's non-violence · Hitler's authoritarianism
Central Traits — most people have 5-10
General characteristics that describe personality · How people describe each other
Ex: honest, aggressive, kind, intelligent
Secondary Traits — peripheral, situational
Appear only in specific contexts · Not central to who you are
Ex: Gets nervous during presentations · Dislikes specific foods

Robbins' Key Personality Attributes

5 PERSONALITY ATTRIBUTES (Robbins)
Locus of Control
Internal LOC: believe they control own outcomes → higher satisfaction, better at complex tasks
External LOC: believe luck/others control outcomes → less initiative
Machiavellianism
High Mach = pragmatic, manipulative, emotionally detached · "End justifies means" · Good in negotiation, persuasion roles
Self-Efficacy (Bandura)
Belief in own ability to succeed at a specific task · Higher self-efficacy → better performance
Self-Monitoring
High SM = adjusts behaviour to social cues, adaptable · Low SM = consistent regardless of situation
Risk-Taking
High risk-takers = faster decisions with less information · Important for entrepreneurship

Type A vs Type B Personality

Type A
Friedman & Rosenman · 1950s
  • Always in a hurry, time-pressured
  • Highly competitive
  • Multitasks constantly
  • Workaholic, aggressive, impatient
  • Higher heart disease risk
  • Works fast: quantity over quality
Type B
Relaxed · Creative · Low stress
  • Relaxed, unhurried
  • Less competitive
  • One thing at a time
  • Easy-going, not aggressive
  • Lower health risk
  • Higher quality, longer-term work
Exam note: Type A = good in crisis, poor at sustainable long-term work · Type B = better for creative projects · Type A = higher risk of burnout

Part 11 — Perception

Definition · 7 Errors + Selective Perception · Attribution Theory
Plain English
Two people see the same thing and interpret it differently. That's perception. Your brain doesn't show you reality — it shows you a filtered, interpreted version of it. Halo = one good thing makes everything look good. Horn = one bad thing taints everything. Contrast = you look amazing because the person before you was terrible.
PERCEPTION — 3 Steps
① Selection
Brain selects what to pay attention to from overwhelming stimuli
② Organisation
Brain groups and organises selected stimuli into patterns
③ Interpretation
Brain gives meaning to organised patterns based on past experience, beliefs, values
7 PERCEPTUAL ERRORS + Selective Perception
① Halo Effect
One POSITIVE trait → positive overall impression. "If good at X, must be good at everything." Ex: IIT grad assumed excellent overall
② Horn Effect (opposite of Halo)
One NEGATIVE trait → negative overall impression. Ex: Poor grammar = assumed incompetent overall
③ Stereotyping
Judging a person based on GROUP membership (gender, race, caste, region, religion)
④ Similarity / Like Me Effect
Favour people similar to ourselves in background, interests, values. Ex: Promote someone from same college
⑤ Contrast Effect
Evaluation affected by comparison with others encountered recently. Ex: Average candidate seems excellent after interviewing weak candidates
⑥ Illusion vs ⑦ Hallucination
Illusion = false perception of REAL stimulus (mirage, bent stick in water)
Hallucination = perception with NO external stimulus (seeing/hearing things that don't exist)
+ Selective Perception
Filter out information conflicting with existing beliefs/interests. Ex: Manager hears only positive feedback, ignores criticism
ATTRIBUTION THEORY
We explain behaviour by attributing causes
Internal attribution = personal factors (ability, effort) · External attribution = situational factors (luck, difficulty)
Fundamental Attribution Error
Overestimate INTERNAL factors for others' failures · Underestimate situational factors · Apply OPPOSITE for own failures
Projection
Attributing YOUR OWN feelings/traits/emotions onto others
MCQ traps:
Halo vs Horn: Halo = ONE POSITIVE → positive overall | Horn = ONE NEGATIVE → negative overall
Contrast vs Selective: Contrast = comparison with OTHER people | Selective = filtering based on OWN beliefs
Illusion vs Hallucination: Illusion has a REAL (but misinterpreted) stimulus | Hallucination has NO real stimulus

Part 12 — Practice MCQs (30 Questions)

Cover options · Answer first · Click to reveal
Q1. According to Fayol, which principle states that an employee should receive orders from only one superior? (a) Unity of Direction (b) Unity of Command (c) Scalar Chain (d) Esprit de Corps
Answer: (b) Unity of Command
Unity of Command = one boss per employee (individual level). Unity of Direction = one plan per group of activities (org level). Common exam trap: students confuse these two.
Q2. "Gang Plank" is associated with which of Fayol's principles? (a) Unity of Command (b) Scalar Chain (c) Discipline (d) Division of Work
Answer: (b) Scalar Chain
Gang Plank = horizontal communication between same-level managers when urgency requires bypassing the scalar chain. It's an exception Fayol allowed within Scalar Chain.
Q3. Which management theory is associated with the "Hawthorne Effect"? (a) Classical Theory (b) Scientific Management (c) Human Relations Theory (d) Systems Theory
Answer: (c) Human Relations Theory
Elton Mayo's Hawthorne Studies at Western Electric led to Neo-Classical / Human Relations Theory. Finding: being observed increases productivity = Hawthorne Effect.
Q4. POSDCORB was given by: (a) Henri Fayol (b) F.W. Taylor (c) Luther Gulick (d) Koontz & O'Donnell
Answer: (c) Luther Gulick
Gulick = POSDCORB (7 functions). Fayol = POCCC (5 functions). Koontz = POSDC (5 functions). Always remember who gave which acronym.
Q5. Which principle of Fayol states "a place for everything and everything in its place"? (a) Equity (b) Initiative (c) Order (d) Unity of Direction
Answer: (c) Order
Order has two parts: Material Order (physical things in right place) and Social Order (right person in right job). The quote = Material Order.
Q6. Taylor's technique involving 8 specialists instead of one foreman is called: (a) Time Study (b) Differential Piece Rate (c) Functional Foremanship (d) Motion Study
Answer: (c) Functional Foremanship
Taylor replaced one all-knowing foreman with 8 specialists (4 planning + 4 production). This violated Fayol's Unity of Command but increased specialisation.
Q7. Nudge Theory was proposed by: (a) Peter Drucker (b) Herbert Simon (c) Richard Thaler & Cass Sunstein (d) William Ouchi
Answer: (c) Richard Thaler & Cass Sunstein
Thaler & Sunstein published "Nudge" in 2008. Thaler won Nobel Prize in Economics 2017 for contributions to behavioural economics including nudge theory.
Q8. Which is a HARD element in McKinsey 7S framework? (a) Skills (b) Style (c) Strategy (d) Shared Values
Answer: (c) Strategy
Hard elements (3S): Strategy, Structure, Systems. Soft elements (4S): Shared Values, Skills, Style, Staff. Trick: "hard" ≠ more important — Shared Values is at the centre.
Q9. Which approach to management says "there is no single best way to manage"? (a) Classical (b) Systems (c) Contingency (d) Behavioural
Answer: (c) Contingency
Contingency = "it depends on the situation." Lawrence & Lorsch, Joan Woodward are key contributors. No universal principles — adapt to environment, technology, people.
Q10. MBO (Management by Objectives) was introduced by: (a) Elton Mayo (b) Henri Fayol (c) Peter Drucker (d) Douglas McGregor
Answer: (c) Peter Drucker
Drucker introduced MBO in "The Practice of Management" (1954). Key: individual and organisational objectives must be aligned; employees participate in setting their own goals.
Q11. A CEO handling a major labour strike falls under which Mintzberg role? (a) Entrepreneur (b) Negotiator (c) Disturbance Handler (d) Resource Allocator
Answer: (c) Disturbance Handler
Disturbance Handler = reactive, involuntary crises (strikes, supplier failures, competitor actions). Negotiator = planned negotiation. Entrepreneur = proactive change-making.
Q12. A CEO giving a press conference to announce quarterly results is performing which role? (a) Figurehead (b) Liaison (c) Spokesperson (d) Disseminator
Answer: (c) Spokesperson
Spokesperson = transmits info to EXTERNAL parties. Figurehead = ceremonial/symbolic (no information). Disseminator = transmits info INTERNALLY to subordinates. Liaison = external networking, not formal communication.
Q13. In the Delphi technique, which is TRUE? (a) Members meet face-to-face (b) Ideas generated without criticism (c) Experts respond anonymously to questionnaires (d) Inner group discusses while outer observes
Answer: (c) Experts respond to questionnaires anonymously
Delphi = remote, anonymous, iterative questionnaires until consensus. (b) = Brainstorming. (d) = Fish Bowl technique.
Q14. Herbert Simon's concept of "satisficing" is associated with: (a) Classical economic rationality (b) Bounded rationality (c) Scientific management (d) Contingency theory
Answer: (b) Bounded rationality
Simon (Nobel 1978) argued decision-makers have bounded (limited) rationality. Therefore they "satisfice" (choose first good-enough option) rather than optimise.
Q15. PERT differs from CPM in that PERT: (a) Identifies the critical path (b) Uses deterministic time estimates (c) Uses three time estimates for uncertain activities (d) Is used in construction projects
Answer: (c) Uses three time estimates for uncertain activities
PERT uses O, M, P time estimates → weighted average. Used in R&D. CPM uses one deterministic estimate, used in construction.
Q16. Among the Big Five personality traits, which is the BEST predictor of job performance? (a) Extraversion (b) Openness (c) Conscientiousness (d) Agreeableness
Answer: (c) Conscientiousness
Conscientiousness (organised, disciplined, reliable) is the strongest and most consistent predictor of job performance across ALL occupations. High-C individuals achieve more and follow through on commitments.
Q17. Type A and Type B personality types were identified by: (a) Allport & Odbert (b) McCrae & Costa (c) Friedman & Rosenman (d) Maslow & Herzberg
Answer: (c) Friedman & Rosenman
Cardiologists Friedman and Rosenman identified Type A/B in the 1950s while studying heart disease risk factors. Type A = time-pressured, competitive, higher heart disease risk.
Q18. A manager giving high ratings simply because a candidate attended a prestigious university is exhibiting: (a) Stereotyping (b) Halo Effect (c) Contrast Effect (d) Horn Effect
Answer: (b) Halo Effect
Halo Effect = one positive characteristic (prestigious university) creates overall positive impression applied to ALL traits. Stereotyping = judging based on group membership, not a specific attribute of this individual.
Q19. "Seeing things that don't exist in reality" is: (a) Illusion (b) Halo Effect (c) Hallucination (d) Selective Perception
Answer: (c) Hallucination
Hallucination = perception without real external stimulus. Illusion = misinterpretation of REAL external stimulus (e.g., mirage = real heat waves but brain interprets as water). Key: hallucination has NO real stimulus.
Q20. An interviewer is more impressed by a mediocre candidate after interviewing several very poor candidates. This is: (a) Halo Effect (b) Stereotyping (c) Contrast Effect (d) Selective Perception
Answer: (c) Contrast Effect
Contrast Effect = evaluation influenced by comparison with recently encountered others. The mediocre candidate seems better only in CONTRAST to weak candidates.
Q21. Which Big Five trait is associated with being anxious, moody and emotionally unstable? (a) Low Extraversion (b) Low Agreeableness (c) High Neuroticism (d) Low Openness
Answer: (c) High Neuroticism
High Neuroticism = emotional instability, anxiety, moodiness. High N = lower job performance. Low N (emotional stability) is desirable in leadership and high-stress roles.
Q22. Hawthorne Studies were conducted at: (a) Ford Motor Company (b) Midvale Steel Company (c) Western Electric Company (d) Bethlehem Steel
Answer: (c) Western Electric Company
Hawthorne Works, Western Electric Company, Chicago (1924-1932). Midvale Steel = where Taylor worked. Bethlehem Steel = where Taylor did pig iron experiments.
Q23. Which Fayol principle was VIOLATED by Taylor's Functional Foremanship? (a) Unity of Direction (b) Scalar Chain (c) Unity of Command (d) Division of Work
Answer: (c) Unity of Command
Unity of Command = one boss per employee. Taylor's Functional Foremanship gave each worker 8 bosses (specialists). This is one of the key criticisms of Taylor's approach from a Fayolian perspective.
Q24. A high Machiavellian person is BEST described as: (a) Insecure and emotionally volatile (b) Pragmatic, manipulative, ends justify means (c) Overly cooperative and trusting (d) Resistant to change and conventional
Answer: (b) Pragmatic, manipulative, and believing ends justify means
Named after Niccolò Machiavelli's "The Prince." High Mach = manipulative, detached, does whatever it takes to achieve goals. (a) = High Neuroticism. (c) = High Agreeableness. (d) = Low Openness.
Q25. Allport's Cardinal Traits are BEST described as: (a) Common traits in all people (b) Situational traits in specific contexts (c) Dominant traits that define the entire personality (d) Workplace-specific traits
Answer: (c) Dominant traits that define the entire personality
Cardinal traits are the most pervasive — define the whole person. Very few people have one. Central traits = 5-10 general characteristics. Secondary = situational, peripheral.

15-Marker Template — Fayol's 14 Principles (2023 PYQ)
Introduction (30–40 words)

Henri Fayol, widely regarded as the Father of Modern Management, propounded 14 universal principles in his work "Administration Industrielle et Générale" (1916). These principles provide a comprehensive framework for effective organisational management applicable across all types of organisations.

Body — 14 Principles (~200 words)

List all 14 with one-line explanation each. Elaborate on 5-6 key ones: Unity of Command (vs Unity of Direction — common trap), Scalar Chain (with Gang Plank), Equity (fairness), Esprit de Corps (team spirit), Centralisation (situational balance).

Relevance to Modern Management (80–100 words)

Despite being formulated a century ago, Fayol's principles remain highly relevant. Unity of Command aligns with matrix organisation debates. Centralisation vs decentralisation remains a strategic choice in flat vs hierarchical orgs. Esprit de Corps underpins modern employee engagement. However, rigid scalar chain has been replaced by agile communication. Principles serve as guidelines, not absolute laws — Fayol's own view: management is an art as much as a science.

Conclusion (20–30 words)

Fayol's 14 principles constitute a foundational framework guiding management practice globally, offering both structure and flexibility for diverse organisational contexts.

The Big Five Personality Model (Five Factor Model/FFM), developed by McCrae and Costa, is the most empirically validated framework for understanding human personality. It identifies five broad dimensions comprehensively capturing personality variation across individuals.

5 Traits — OCEAN (~150 words)
